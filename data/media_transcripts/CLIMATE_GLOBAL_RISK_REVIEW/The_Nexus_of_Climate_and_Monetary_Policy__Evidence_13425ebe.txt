Well to start I like to briefly highlight the main takeaways of our paper which is structured around two key pillars a theoretical framework and an empirical model First some key insights from the theoretical side When we think about monetary policy, the central tool is the policy rate. Adjusting this rate activates the so-called transmission mechanism of monetary policy, influencing aggregate demand, especially in sectors sensitive to interest rates. That said, one of the important observations is that monetary policy tends to have a more muted effect on the volatile components of inflation, namely food prices. This presents a significant challenge as it limits the scope of traditional monetary policy to fully tackle all inflationary pressures. And this is precisely where climate policy comes in. For example, policies that improve access to water for agriculture can significantly reduce food price volatility by directly addressing One of the key drivers of inflation that monetary policy struggles to contain, these interventions broaden the toolkit available to policymakers. From a macroeconomic perspective, we see that monetary and climate policies are not just complementary. They are mutually reinforcing. While monetary policy focuses on non-food inflation, climate policy helps to stabilize the more volatile food price component. Together, they allow central banks to achieve more effectively the core mandate, price stability. Now, with this theoretical understanding in mind, we applied the framework to the Middle East and Central Asia, regions particularly vulnerable to climate shocks, as Nordin pointed out earlier. Our empirical analysis tests the framework and the two scenarios, one with favorable climate conditions and another one with unfavorable climate conditions. And here is how we approached it. we looked at the impact of an unexpected monetary tightening, a 100 basis point increase in the policy rate, on inflation, under favourable climate conditions. And you can think about it as a situation where water availability exceeded expectations and supported agriculture. We found that inflation decreased significantly by about 1 to 1.5 percentage points over the next 2 to 3 years, a result that is statistically significant. However, under unfavourable climate conditions, when water stress was acute, the same monetary tightening had a much smaller effect. Inflation decreased by only 0.2 to 0.4 percentage points, and the result is not statistically significant. What does this tell us? It clearly shows that monetary policy is more effective when climate conditions are favorable. This shows the complementarity, the nexus between monetary and climate policies. So what are the key takeaways here? First, monetary policy has limitations, particularly in economies where food prices account for a large share of inflation. Second, climate policy plays an important role in complementing monetary policy. By building resilience, especially in the agricultural sector, climate policy helps mitigate inflationary pressure from volatile food prices. Finally, policymakers must fully use the tools at their disposal. For instance, the IMF Resilience and Sustainability Facility, the RSF, offers essential support to countries facing climate-related challenges. All in all, we show in this paper that by integrating monetary and climate policies, policymakers can better tackle economic shocks and ensure stronger price stability.
